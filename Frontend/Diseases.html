Citrus Diseases

1. HLB

Huanglongbing (HLB), often known as citrus greening, is a bacterial infection of citrus plants. Candidatus Liberibacter is the bacterium that causes this illness. Sucking insects and plant migration between different countries and continents are two ways that the bacteria propagate over the diverse range of citrus trees. The Asian Citrus Psyllid (Diaphorina citri) is an insect that spreads this disease. In citrus trees, the disease hinders the movement of nutrients. Citrus trees are slowly killed by it. The disease initially appears as mottling of the leaves, which is followed by short shoots, a slow death of the branches, and finally small, malformed fruits with bitter juice.

2. Citrus Canker

The "Citrus Canker" bacterial disease affects citrus trees and causes harm to crops as well as trade disruptions. It is brought on by the bacteria Xanthomonas Axonopodis pv. citri. The signs of citrus canker include yellow halo and raised, brown lesions with water-soaked edges on fruit, leaves, or stems. Citrus Canker primarily causes spots on the leaves and imperfections on the fruit's skin. However, when the right circumstances are met for the illness to spread, more severe problems including leaves dropping off the tree, branches withering back, and fruits falling off the tree before they are fully formed might ensue. Splashing rain, insects, and wind are the main ways that these spreads. Insects like aphids and citrus leaf miners that feed on diseased plants can also carry the bacteria, as can water droplets from irrigation or rainfall.

3. Citrus Sooty Mold

A fungus causes citrus sooty mold, a black, powdery coating that appears on the leaves, stems, and fruit of citrus plants. Mold grows in the sugary waste products known as honeydew left behind by aphids, whiteflies, and scale insects. The plant is not harmed by the mold itself. This opaque layer may block sunlight, which reduces the plant's ability to use photosynthetic energy and weakens the plant, ultimately resulting in poor development and fruit. One way to help manage the disease is to reduce the number of insects and remove any mold.

4. Citrus Scab

Citrus scab is a fungal disease that forms rough, corky lesions on citrus plants' leaves, twigs, and fruit. It primarily affects developing, immature tissues. The fungus that causes the illness is called Elsinoë fawcettii. The lesions start off as little, raised patches of pale orange color that progressively darken and form scabs or moles. The scab usually has no effect on the fruit's inner quality, though it may become less appealing and marketable. Leaf infection might also cause distortion. It is spread by wind, rain and splashing water. The contaminated soil along with the plant debris can also be the source of fungal spores which spreads the disease. The illness is managed with fungicides, and in order to prevent the fungus from spreading, affected plant material needs to be removed.

5. Citrus Black Spot

The fungus that causes Citrus Black Spot, also known as Guignardia citricarpa, Phyllosticta citricarpa, is responsible for the dark, sunken patches on fruit that reduce its marketability and aesthetic appeal. The disease primarily affects fruit, but in severe cases, twigs and leaves may also show symptoms. When the spots, which usually have a yellow halo surrounding them, become hard, raised lesions, the fruit is no longer appropriate for sale. In severe cases, citrus black spot can cause early fruit loss, which reduces yield. The fungus spreads by means of sick plant material and spores carried by the wind or rain which carries the spores from one plant to another. Management strategies include applying fungicides, removing fallen fruit and leaves, and enforcing strict quarantine laws in order to prevent the illness from spreading.
